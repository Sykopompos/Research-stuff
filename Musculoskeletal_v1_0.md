**Abstract**

The musculoskeletal system comprises two endocrine organs — skeletal
muscle and bone — whose physiological role includes, alongside
mechanical support and locomotion, the transduction of behavioral inputs
(exercise, mechanical load) into biological age signals distributed
systemically. Skeletal muscle is the body’s largest metabolic organ,
accounting for approximately 30–40% of body mass in lean adults and 70%
of insulin-stimulated glucose uptake; it communicates exercise state to
the brain, adipose tissue, and liver through myokines (irisin, IL-6,
FGF21) and the neurotrophin BDNF released during sustained exercise.
Bone is a dynamic endocrine organ secreting osteocalcin
(osteoblast-derived; targets brain memory circuits, pancreatic β-cells,
and skeletal muscle mitochondria) and FGF23 (osteocyte-derived;
regulates renal phosphate excretion and calcitriol synthesis). These two
organs are coupled through the osteocalcin–myokine feedback loop, shared
anabolic hormonal regulation (GH/IGF-1, testosterone, estradiol), and
the mechanostat, in which mechanical loading drives osteoblast
activation and muscle hypertrophy simultaneously.

This paper applies the five-operation decomposition to skeletal muscle
and bone and analyzes three coupled failure cascades: the sedentary
aging cascade (physical inactivity → AMPK under-stimulation →
mitochondrial density loss → insulin resistance → sarcopenia → reduced
osteocalcin → cognitive decline), the osteoporosis–fracture–mortality
cascade (sex hormone loss → RANKL/OPG imbalance → trabecular thinning →
hip fracture → immobilization → pneumonia), and the GLP-1 receptor
agonist–lean mass interaction (pharmacologic weight loss → lean mass
loss of 25–40% of total weight lost, partially mitigated by resistance
training and protein intake). The exercise-aging-cognition axis is the
paper’s defining claim: physical activity is among the most effective
evidence-based interventions for depression, insulin resistance,
cognitive aging, and sarcopenia simultaneously, operating through
converging molecular mechanisms (BDNF, irisin, osteocalcin,
lactate–HCAR1–angiogenesis) that no single pharmacological agent
currently activates as broadly, to our knowledge. Evidence tiers (A:
replicated, mechanistically confirmed; B: mechanistically supported; C:
integrative synthesis) are assigned throughout.

**§1 — The Musculoskeletal System as a Coupled Endocrine Architecture**

Skeletal muscle and bone are textbook examples of mechanical tissues,
and their roles in force generation and structural support are among the
best-characterized functions in physiology. This framing is correct but
incomplete. Both tissues function simultaneously as endocrine organs:
skeletal muscle secretes a constellation of myokines during contraction
that communicate exercise state to the brain, adipose tissue, liver, and
immune system; bone secretes osteocalcin, a hormone that modulates brain
memory circuits, pancreatic β-cell insulin secretion, and skeletal
muscle mitochondrial function, with circulating levels rising with
exercise and mechanical loading \[3\]. These endocrine functions
represent a major and underemphasized physiological output of both
tissues: endocrine signaling that translates behavioral inputs into
systemic metabolic and aging trajectories.

The AMPK–PGC-1α axis is the molecular core of the exercise-transduction
system. During contraction, muscle ATP is consumed faster than it is
regenerated, raising the AMP/ATP ratio. AMPK (AMP-activated protein
kinase) senses this ratio and, when activated, simultaneously triggers
GLUT4 translocation to the plasma membrane (insulin-independent glucose
uptake), β-oxidation upregulation, and PGC-1α (peroxisome
proliferator-activated receptor γ coactivator 1-α) transcription. PGC-1α
is the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis: a single bout of
exercise activates PGC-1α transiently; repeated bouts over weeks
generate durable increases in mitochondrial density, oxidative capacity,
and myokine secretory capacity \[1\]. The trainability of this axis —
its ability to encode chronic exercise patterns into durable structural
changes — is the mechanistic basis of exercise’s broad therapeutic
effects \[1\].

The bone–muscle axis operates bidirectionally. Muscle contraction
applies mechanical loads to bone that activate osteoblasts through the
mechanostat (Frost 1987): loading strains exceeding a minimum effective
strain threshold stimulate periosteal bone formation, while disuse
produces net resorption \[2\]. Bone, in turn, secretes osteocalcin whose
exercise-induced rise feeds back to skeletal muscle to enhance
mitochondrial function and exercise capacity \[3\]. This loop means that
exercise simultaneously builds muscle (AMPK → PGC-1α) and bone
(mechanostat → osteoblast), while the improved osteocalcin signal from
bone further enhances muscle performance — a self-amplifying adaptive
program that physical inactivity progressively dismantles.

The brain-musculoskeletal interface is the paper’s defining claim and
the justification for this paper’s standalone status. Exercise generates
at least four classes of brain-active signals: irisin (FNDC5 cleavage
product; crosses the blood-brain barrier; promotes hippocampal
neuroplasticity via αV integrin receptors) \[4, 5\]; BDNF (both
centrally synthesized during exercise and peripherally released from
contracting muscle as pro-BDNF that is cleaved to mature BDNF in
circulation) \[6\]; lactate (crosses the blood-brain barrier via
monocarboxylate transporters; activates HCAR1, induces angiogenesis, and
augments BDNF release through SIRT1–PGC-1α–FNDC5 pathway) \[7\]; and
osteocalcin (bone-derived; crosses the blood-brain barrier; binds GPR158
on hippocampal neurons; enhances synthesis of monoamine
neurotransmitters, BDNF, and synaptic plasticity proteins) \[8, 9\]. No
single pharmacological agent activates all four pathways simultaneously.
This convergence explains why exercise is consistently identified as
among the most effective interventions for depression, cognitive aging,
and metabolic syndrome in the published evidence base \[10\].

The five-operation decomposition used throughout this paper is an
analytical scaffold for locating control logic and identifying coupling
points. The coupling analysis in §3 identifies the shared interfaces;
the failure cascade analysis in §4 shows how those interfaces amplify
rather than correct failure when physical activity is chronically
reduced.

Sources for this review were drawn from primary peer-reviewed
literature, prioritizing mechanistic studies, landmark clinical trials,
and recent literature (2023–2025) in areas of active development (irisin

**§2.1 — Skeletal Muscle**

**Propagation.** Skeletal muscle propagates both mechanical force and
metabolic signals. The contraction cascade initiates at the
neuromuscular junction: motor neuron action potential → acetylcholine
release → nAChR activation → end-plate potential → T-tubule
depolarization → DHPR (dihydropyridine receptor) → RyR1 (ryanodine
receptor 1) opening → Ca²⁺ release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum →
troponin C → actin-myosin cross-bridge cycling → force. ATP consumption
during this cycle raises the AMP/ATP ratio within seconds, activating
AMPK. Activated AMPK propagates three parallel signals: GLUT4 vesicle
translocation to the plasma membrane (enabling insulin-independent
glucose uptake), acetyl-CoA carboxylase phosphorylation (enabling
malonyl-CoA suppression → CPT-1 activation → β-oxidation), and
CaMKII-mediated MEF2 activation → PGC-1α gene expression → mitochondrial
biogenesis \[1\]. Exercise-induced myokine secretion is an additional
propagation layer: FNDC5 is cleaved to release irisin into the
circulation; IL-6 is released from contracting fibers in proportion to
glycogen depletion; BDNF is expressed as pro-BDNF (a neurotrophin rather
than a classical myokine) in type I fibers and released during exercise
with subsequent cleavage to mature BDNF in circulation \[6\].

**Gating.** The primary anabolic gate in skeletal muscle is the
AMPK–mTOR opposition. mTORC1 (mechanistic target of rapamycin complex 1)
is the master anabolic gate: activated by amino acids (via
Ragulator/GATOR complex), insulin/IGF-1 (via IRS-1 → PI3K → AKT → TSC2
phosphorylation), and mechanical load (via focal adhesion kinase), it
phosphorylates S6K1 and 4EBP1 to drive ribosomal biogenesis and protein
synthesis. AMPK opposes mTOR under energy deficit by phosphorylating
TSC2 (activating it) and Raptor (inhibiting mTORC1 directly): the cell
cannot build protein when energy-depleted. This AMPK–mTOR gate is the
molecular basis of the anabolic window after resistance exercise, when
acute AMPK activation is followed by mTOR-driven protein synthesis as
energy is restored. The second gate is myostatin: a TGF-β family member
secreted by muscle fibers that signals via SMAD2/3 to suppress satellite
cell proliferation and mTOR-driven hypertrophy. Myostatin increases with
aging and in disease cachexia, producing anabolic resistance — the
inability to build muscle despite adequate stimulus and substrate \[1\].

**Detection.** Skeletal muscle is both a sensor and an effector. AMPK
functions as the primary intracellular metabolic sensor, detecting
AMP/ATP ratio. The intramuscular lipid accumulation sensor is the
DAG–PKC-θ pathway: excess intramyocellular lipid (IMCL) generates
diacylglycerol (DAG), activating PKC-θ, which phosphorylates IRS-1 at
Ser307, impairing insulin signal propagation to GLUT4 — this pathway
constitutes the primary molecular mechanism of skeletal muscle insulin
resistance. Proprioceptive detection operates through two peripheral
sensor populations: muscle spindles (intrafusal fibers detecting length
and stretch rate; afferent via Ia/II fibers → cerebellum and motor
cortex) and Golgi tendon organs (GTO; detecting tension; afferent via Ib
fibers → spinal inhibitory interneurons) \[12\]; detailed proprioceptor
circuit architecture is covered in PeripheralNS v0.8. The
Ca²⁺/calmodulin-dependent kinase II (CaMKII) additionally functions as a
frequency-sensitive integrator of contractile Ca²⁺ transients,
translating exercise intensity into gene regulatory signals \[1\].

**Accumulation.** Chronic disuse produces sarcopenia: progressive loss
of muscle mass and strength at approximately 1–2% per year after age 50,
accelerating after 70 \[25\]. The mechanism involves reduced satellite
cell number and activity, anabolic resistance to amino acid and insulin
signaling (Tier A), elevated myostatin (Tier B), and reduced neuromotor
unit recruitment. Sarcopenia reduces glucose disposal capacity (since
skeletal muscle accounts for ~70% of insulin-stimulated uptake),
directly worsening insulin resistance independent of adiposity. Chronic
elevated cortisol (HPA axis activation, cachexia) activates the
ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS) in muscle fibers, driving net protein
catabolism. Lipid accumulation within muscle fibers (intramyocellular
lipid, IMCL) further promotes insulin resistance through the DAG–PKC-θ
pathway, creating a self-amplifying accumulation state: inactivity →
IMCL ↑ → insulin resistance → further impaired muscle glucose metabolism
\[1\].

**Refinement.** Skeletal muscle is a remarkably trainable organ.
Exercise training drives durable increases in mitochondrial density
(Tier A), GLUT4 expression (Tier A), capillary density, and oxidative
enzyme capacity over weeks. Type I slow-twitch fibers (oxidative)
increase their mitochondrial volume density with endurance training;
type II fast-twitch fibers hypertrophy with resistance training via
mTOR-driven protein synthesis. Exercise-induced reduction of myostatin
mRNA expression reduces the anabolic suppression threshold, enabling
greater hypertrophic response per unit stimulus — a training-specific
recalibration. The exercise-induced increase in irisin, BDNF, and
osteocalcin secretory capacity provides an additional adaptive benefit
that extends refinement beyond the muscle to brain and bone \[4, 5, 6\].

**§2.2 — Bone**

**Propagation.** Bone propagates mechanical and hormonal signals. The
mechanostat (Frost 1987) describes the bone remodeling response to
mechanical strain: strains exceeding the minimum effective strain
threshold activate osteocytes (the primary mechanosensors embedded in
the bone matrix), which suppress sclerostin secretion; reduced
sclerostin releases WNT signaling inhibition, activating osteoblast
differentiation and bone formation \[2\]. Below the remodeling
threshold, sclerostin expression increases, suppressing bone formation.
This mechanostat couples physical loading directly to bone mass
accumulation: weight-bearing exercise is the primary stimulus for bone
formation at any age. Bone also propagates endocrine signals:
osteocalcin (secreted by osteoblasts as a non-collagenous matrix
protein; undercarboxylated osteocalcin, ucOC, is the bioactive form)
enters circulation and acts on skeletal muscle (GPRC6A receptors), brain
(GPR158 → monoamine synthesis + BDNF ↑), and pancreatic β-cells (→
insulin secretion ↑) \[3, 8\]; FGF23 (secreted by osteocytes) acts on
renal tubules to suppress 1α-hydroxylase, reducing calcitriol synthesis
\[26\].

**Gating.** The primary resorption gate in bone is the RANKL/OPG system.
Osteoblasts and osteocytes secrete RANKL (receptor activator of NF-κB
ligand), which binds RANK receptors on osteoclast precursors, driving
osteoclast differentiation, fusion, and bone resorption. Osteoprotegerin
(OPG), also secreted by osteoblasts, functions as a decoy receptor that
sequesters RANKL and prevents osteoclast activation. The RANKL/OPG ratio
is the primary gate of net bone resorption: estradiol (E2) drives OPG
expression and suppresses RANKL, providing the hormonal basis of bone
protection in premenopausal women; E2 loss at menopause shifts the ratio
toward RANKL, initiating accelerated bone loss \[13\]. PTH drives
osteoclast activation by suppressing OPG and increasing RANKL on
osteoblasts; intermittent PTH (teriparatide) stimulates net bone
formation — in contrast to the resorptive effects of continuously
elevated PTH — by activating the PKA/CREB pathway and suppressing
sclerostin expression in osteocytes \[16, 27\]. The second gate is the
Wnt/LRP5–6/β-catenin anabolic gate: activation (by mechanical loading,
pharmacological sclerostin inhibition) drives osteoblast
differentiation; inhibition (by sclerostin, DKK1, myostatin) suppresses
it \[16\].

**Detection.** Osteocytes are the primary mechanosensors and act as the
bone’s distributed sensory network: embedded within the
lacunocanalicular network at a density of approximately 25,000 cells/mm³
\[21\], they detect strain via primary cilia and integrin-matrix
connections, transduce mechanical signals through fluid flow-induced
shear stress, and communicate via gap junctions to surface osteoblasts
and osteoclasts. Systemic detection is achieved through calcium sensing:
the calcium-sensing receptor (CaSR) on parathyroid chief cells detects
ionized Ca²⁺ in blood; hypocalcemia → PTH secretion → osteoclast
activation + renal Ca²⁺ reabsorption + calcitriol synthesis → intestinal
Ca²⁺ absorption ↑. Phosphate sensing is through FGF23: elevated serum
phosphate (diet or reduced renal excretion) → FGF23 ↑ from osteocytes →
renal phosphate excretion ↑ + calcitriol ↓. The bone thus functions as a
distributed phosphate and calcium sensor with systemic feedback loops to
kidney and gut.

**Accumulation.** Bone loss accumulates silently until fracture
threshold is crossed. Peak bone mass is achieved in the third decade;
after age 30 there is a slow net annual loss of approximately 0.5–1% BMD
per year, accelerating to 2–3% per year in the first five years
post-menopause due to estrogen withdrawal. Trabecular bone (vertebrae,
hip, distal radius) is preferentially lost because its high surface
area:volume ratio exposes more surface to osteoclast resorption.
Trabecular thinning, perforation, and loss of connectivity reduce bone
strength beyond what BMD alone predicts: trabecular connectivity and
thickness contribute more to fracture resistance than mineral density,
such that architectural deterioration amplifies mechanical failure
disproportionately to the measured BMD reduction \[16\]. Cortical bone
is lost more slowly but contributes to hip fracture risk through
endosteal thinning. The accumulation trajectory is strongly influenced
by calcium and vitamin D intake (required for adequate bone
mineralization), sex hormones, and mechanical loading history.

**Refinement.** Bone retains substantial adaptive capacity. The
mechanostat is directly trainable: weight-bearing exercise increases BMD
at loaded sites in premenopausal women and attenuates bone loss in
postmenopausal women (Tier A). Bisphosphonates (alendronate,
risedronate, zoledronic acid) reduce bone resorption by inhibiting
osteoclast farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase, suppressing the mevalonate
pathway and preventing osteoclast survival; 3–5 year treatment reduces
hip fracture risk by approximately 30–40% (Tier A) \[16\]. Denosumab
(anti-RANKL monoclonal antibody) suppresses RANKL-mediated osteoclast
activation; discontinuation risks rebound vertebral fractures and, less
commonly, transient hypercalcemia. Romosozumab (anti-sclerostin) is the
first approved anabolic bone agent that simultaneously inhibits
resorption and stimulates formation, producing net BMD gains of 13–15%
at the spine over one year (FRAME trial) \[22\]. The anabolic window for
exercise-bone adaptation is lifelong, though the response magnitude is
largest in childhood and adolescence.

**§3.1 — The Osteocalcin–Myokine Feedback Loop**

The osteocalcin–myokine axis is the primary bidirectional endocrine
coupling between muscle and bone. During exercise, muscle contractions
apply mechanical load to bone, activating osteoblasts through the
mechanostat. Activated osteoblasts secrete osteocalcin; circulating
undercarboxylated osteocalcin (ucOC) acts on muscle fibers through
GPRC6A receptors to upregulate mitochondrial gene expression and enhance
exercise capacity — a closed-loop system in which the muscle’s
mechanical output drives a bone-derived hormonal signal that feeds back
to improve muscle metabolic performance \[3, 4\]. This loop means that
skeletal muscle fitness and bone endocrine function are co-regulated:
the sedentary individual loses both simultaneously, and exercise
restores both simultaneously.

The myokine-to-bone direction operates primarily through irisin and
exercise-context IL-6. Irisin (cleaved from FNDC5 during exercise) acts
on osteocytes via αV/β5 integrin receptors — the receptor mechanism
confirmed by cryo-EM structural studies in 2023 showing that
extracellular Hsp90α secreted by muscle activates the integrin for
high-affinity irisin binding \[5\]. Irisin modulates osteocyte
sclerostin production, linking exercise intensity directly to WNT
pathway activity in bone. IL-6 released from contracting muscle
(anti-inflammatory context; distinct from the pro-inflammatory chronic
IL-6 of obesity and rheumatoid arthritis) acts on osteoblasts to couple
acute energy demand to bone remodeling signals. This bidirectional
myokine-hormone exchange is the molecular substrate for the
well-established epidemiological finding that sarcopenia and
osteoporosis are tightly correlated beyond their shared hormonal
determinants — a relationship consistent with bidirectional
myokine-hormone coupling \[3\].

**§3.2 — The Exercise–Brain Axis**

The exercise–brain axis operates through four parallel molecular
channels. First, BDNF: skeletal muscle expresses pro-BDNF predominantly
in type I oxidative fibers; exercise increases muscle and plasma
pro-BDNF and, through cleavage, circulating mature BDNF. A 2024 human
study confirmed that simultaneous lactate infusion during resistance
exercise induced a significantly greater increase in plasma mature BDNF
compared to exercise alone, establishing lactate as a direct augmenter
of the BDNF response in humans \[6\]. Circulating BDNF crosses the
blood-brain barrier and acts through TrkB receptors on hippocampal
neurons to drive neurogenesis, synaptogenesis, and long-term
potentiation \[14\].

Second, irisin: exercise-induced irisin crosses the blood-brain barrier
and binds αV integrin receptors on hippocampal neurons. The receptor
mechanism has been established by cryo-EM structural studies \[5\]:
Hsp90α secreted by muscle activates the αVβ5 integrin into a
high-affinity open state, enabling irisin binding at physiological
nanomolar concentrations; without this prior activation step, irisin
cannot engage its receptor effectively. Irisin promotes hippocampal
neuroplasticity and resistance to neurodegeneration in animal models;
human observational studies consistently show lower circulating irisin
in Alzheimer’s disease and depression (Tier B for mechanism; Tier B for
human observational association; Tier C for clinical causation).

Third, lactate: at high exercise intensities, skeletal muscle produces
lactate at rates exceeding hepatic clearance, raising blood lactate to
4–10 mM at moderate-to-high intensities (resting ~1 mM). Lactate crosses
the blood-brain barrier via monocarboxylate transporters (MCT1/2), where
it activates HCAR1 (hydroxycarboxylic acid receptor 1), inducing
cerebral angiogenesis. In hippocampal neurons, lactate activates the
SIRT1–PGC-1α–FNDC5 pathway, upregulating BDNF gene expression. The acute
versus chronic distinction matters: lactate infusion studies show
augmentation of circulating BDNF but more variable direct hippocampal
effects, suggesting chronic lactate exposure from regular exercise may
be more important than acute peaks \[7\]. This is a Tier B mechanistic
claim with Tier C for the causal chain from exercise-intensity to
lasting hippocampal BDNF induction in humans.

Fourth, osteocalcin: circulating osteocalcin crosses the blood-brain
barrier and binds GPR158 receptors on hippocampal neurons, where it
upregulates the synthesis of monoamine neurotransmitters (dopamine,
serotonin, norepinephrine) and BDNF, and promotes autophagy in
hippocampal neurons \[8\]. Age-related decline in osteocalcin levels is
associated with cognitive deterioration in observational studies; a 2024
Mendelian randomization study found a causal relationship between blood
osteocalcin levels and Alzheimer’s disease risk mediated through energy
metabolism \[9\]. No clinical trial has tested osteocalcin
supplementation in humans; the causal relationship at clinical magnitude
remains Tier C pending interventional evidence.

**§3.3 — The Metabolic Interface**

Skeletal muscle is the body’s primary glucose disposal organ, accounting
for approximately 70% of insulin-stimulated whole-body glucose uptake
through GLUT4 transporter translocation to the sarcolemma \[1\].
Exercise activates GLUT4 translocation independently of insulin via
AMPK, providing a non-insulin-dependent glucose disposal mechanism that
remains functional even in advanced insulin resistance. This makes
exercise among the most mechanistically targeted interventions for type
2 diabetes: it bypasses the insulin signaling defect at IRS-1 Ser307
that characterizes skeletal muscle insulin resistance and directly
drives GLUT4 membrane insertion \[17\].

Bone participates in the metabolic interface through osteocalcin’s
effect on pancreatic β-cell insulin secretion. Undercarboxylated
osteocalcin acts through GPRC6A receptors on β-cells to enhance insulin
secretion and through the same receptors on adipocytes to promote
adiponectin secretion (improving hepatic and muscle insulin
sensitivity). The exercise–osteocalcin–β-cell feedback loop thus creates
a non-pancreatic mechanism by which physical activity enhances insulin
secretory capacity. This is Tier B for the mouse mechanistic data \[4\]
with Tier B human epidemiological support; the interventional clinical
chain in humans remains Tier C.

Lactate produced by contracting muscle during high-intensity exercise
enters the Cori cycle: exported to the liver, converted back to glucose
via gluconeogenesis, and returned to muscle. This lactate shuttling is
not merely metabolic waste disposal but a fuel-sharing mechanism: the
brain uses lactate as an alternative energy substrate during exercise,
and the exercise-induced rise in blood lactate provides a substrate
signal that activates HCAR1-mediated cerebral angiogenesis, improving
cerebrovascular reserve over months to years of regular training \[7\].

**§4 — Multi-System Failure Cascades**

The following failure patterns emerge from the musculoskeletal coupling
architecture. Each is an integrative synthesis: the component mechanisms
are supported at the tier indicated, but the cascades as unified
clinical patterns represent proposed connections between individually
supported mechanisms rather than directly measured unified sequences.
Tier designations follow the evidence tiers defined in §1.

**§4.1 — Sedentary Aging: The Metabolic-Cognitive Cascade**

The sedentary aging cascade is Tier A for the individual mechanistic
steps and Tier C as a unified program: the molecular links from AMPK
under-stimulation to insulin resistance to sarcopenia to osteocalcin
decline are each well-supported, but their combined contribution to a
measurable cognitive trajectory in a given individual has not been
demonstrated as a directly measured causal sequence. The cascade
represents a convergence of individually validated mechanisms toward a
common endpoint.

The cascade initiates at Stage 1 with physical inactivity–induced AMPK
under-stimulation. Without contractile activity, AMPK is not chronically
activated; PGC-1α transcription declines; mitochondrial density and
oxidative capacity fall. GLUT4 expression at the sarcolemma decreases,
reducing insulin-stimulated glucose uptake. IMCL accumulates as
β-oxidation capacity is lost, activating DAG–PKC-θ → IRS-1 Ser307
phosphorylation → progressive skeletal muscle insulin resistance (Tier A
for mechanism; Tier A for epidemiological linkage) \[1, 17\].

Stage 2 is the sarcopenia–metabolic spiral. As muscle mass declines at
1–2%/year, total glucose disposal capacity falls proportionally. Reduced
muscle mechanical loading of the skeleton drives net bone resorption
(mechanostat below threshold): BMD declines, osteocalcin secretion falls
with reduced osteoblast activity. Reduced circulating osteocalcin
impairs β-cell insulin secretion and reduces muscle mitochondrial gene
expression \[4\], further reducing exercise capacity and ATP production.
D2R (dopamine D2 receptor) density in the striatum declines with aging
\[18\], reducing the motivational salience of exercise and creating a
behavioral feedback loop: reduced exercise capacity → reduced rewarding
feedback from exercise → further inactivity (Tier B for D2R-motivation
coupling).

Stage 3 is the brain consequence. BDNF production by contracting muscle
declines with inactivity; hippocampal BDNF expression falls.
Osteocalcin–GPR158–BDNF signaling to hippocampal neurons is reduced.
Irisin-mediated neuroplasticity is lost. These converging reductions in
exercise-dependent neurotrophic signals are associated with accelerated
hippocampal volume loss, reduced neurogenesis, and increased depression
and cognitive impairment risk (Tier B for individual
signal-neuroplasticity links; Tier C for the coordinated cascade as a
cognitive aging trajectory) \[14\]. The following reflects a proposed
synthesis: the individually supported signal losses (BDNF, osteocalcin,
irisin) have not been measured as a unified cascade in human
longitudinal studies, but the convergence is consistent with the
well-documented exercise–cognition dose-response relationship: regular
aerobic exercise producing measurable BDNF increases is associated with
meaningfully lower risk of cognitive decline and dementia in
observational cohorts, with meta-analytic support for causal direction
from randomized trials \[10\].

**§4.2 — Osteoporosis–Fracture–Mortality Cascade**

The osteoporosis–fracture–mortality cascade is Tier A throughout: each
step is epidemiologically and mechanistically established, the
interventional evidence is RCT-level, and the outcome data are
population-level.

| **Stage**              | **Mechanism**                                                                                                                        | **Tier** | **Intervention window**                                                                                                        |
|------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| 1: Trabecular thinning | E2 ↓ (menopause) → OPG ↓ → RANKL unchecked → osteoclast activation → trabecular perforations + connectivity loss                     | A        | Anti-resorptive agents (bisphosphonates, denosumab) + weight-bearing exercise; romosozumab (anabolic + anti-resorptive) \[16\] |
| 2: Fall risk           | Sarcopenia → reduced muscle strength + proprioception ↓ (PeripheralNS coupling) → balance impairment; vestibular aging; polypharmacy | A        | Resistance training + balance training + vitamin D + medication review                                                         |
| 3: Hip fracture        | Fall onto greater trochanter → intertrochanteric or femoral neck fracture; trabecular microarchitecture failure                      | A        | Hip protectors (partial evidence); fracture liaison service                                                                    |
| 4: Mortality           | Immobilization → disuse atrophy + DVT/PE + aspiration pneumonia; 20–25% 1-year mortality post-hip-fracture \[15\]                    | A        | Early surgical repair (\<48hr); rehabilitation; secondary prevention                                                           |

*Table 2. Osteoporosis–fracture–mortality cascade with intervention
windows.*

The osteoporosis–fracture–mortality cascade is notable for the
irreversibility of Stage 3: hip fracture initiates immobilization which
accelerates sarcopenia (disuse atrophy) and osteoporosis simultaneously,
creating a self-amplifying terminal trajectory. The 20–25% 1-year
mortality rate from hip fracture — comparable to myocardial infarction —
is driven primarily by complications of immobility (pneumonia, pulmonary
embolism) rather than the fracture itself, underscoring the importance
of surgical repair speed and early rehabilitation \[15\]. The coupling
to sarcopenia means that bone and muscle failure are not sequential but
simultaneous: the same patient who fractures a hip typically has
advanced sarcopenia, which both contributed to the fall and will worsen
dramatically during the post-fracture recovery period.

**§4.3 — GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Therapy and Lean Mass Preservation**

The emergence of highly effective GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide,
tirzepatide) and dual GLP-1/GIP agonists for obesity treatment has
introduced a new clinical challenge at the musculoskeletal interface:
pharmacologic weight loss produces lean mass loss of 25–40% of total
weight lost, with individual variation. A 2024 network meta-analysis of
22 randomized controlled trials (2,258 participants) confirmed that
semaglutide and tirzepatide at their highest approved doses produce the
greatest absolute fat and total weight loss but are among the least
effective in preserving lean mass \[19\]. In the STEP-1 semaglutide
trial, lean mass was reduced by approximately 6.9 kg against a total
weight reduction of 15.3 kg \[23\]; in SURMOUNT-1 tirzepatide, lean mass
reduction comprised approximately 26% of total weight lost at the
highest dose \[24\].

The clinical concern is twofold. First, absolute lean mass reduction at
the scale seen with these agents in individuals already at risk for
sarcopenia (older adults, those with pre-existing low muscle mass) may
accelerate the sedentary aging cascade described in §4.1. Second, lean
mass reduction reduces the body’s primary glucose disposal capacity,
potentially offsetting the metabolic benefits of fat mass reduction.
Whether these changes are adaptive (commensurate with the expected lean
mass reduction at the observed degree of weight loss) or maladaptive
(disproportionate muscle loss beyond predicted allometric scaling) is
currently debated: muscle volume z-scores suggest changes are largely
proportional to weight loss, but the absolute reductions are clinically
meaningful in vulnerable populations \[11\].

Mitigation strategies are emerging at Tier B–C. Resistance training
combined with adequate protein intake (1.2–1.6 g/kg/day) largely
attenuates lean mass loss during GLP-1 therapy in case series with DXA
body composition data; structured exercise 4–7 days per week with 3–5
sessions of resistance training preserved or increased lean soft tissue
mass in three patients losing 13–33% of total body weight \[20\]. The
BELIEVE Phase 2b trial (presented as late-breaking data at the 85th ADA
Scientific Sessions, June 2025; not yet peer-reviewed) demonstrated that
bimagrumab (anti-activin type II receptor antibody) combined with
semaglutide enhanced fat loss while preserving and increasing lean mass
relative to semaglutide alone, representing the first pharmacological
muscle-preservation strategy validated alongside a GLP-1 agent. These
findings are Tier C as a unified clinical management program: individual
mitigation components are supported, but standardized protocols for lean
mass preservation during GLP-1 therapy do not yet exist at RCT scale.
This cascade will require monitoring as GLP-1/GIP therapies become
first-line obesity treatment in populations with pre-existing sarcopenia
risk.

**§5 — The Integrated Musculoskeletal System**

The musculoskeletal system functions as an exercise-transducing
endocrine architecture with four shared coupling interfaces:

- Osteocalcin–myokine feedback loop: exercise → muscle contraction →
  mechanical loading of bone → osteocalcin ↑ → muscle mitochondrial
  function ↑ + brain memory circuits + β-cell insulin secretion; irisin
  acts in reverse (muscle → bone via αVβ5 integrin) to modulate
  sclerostin and WNT signaling

- Mechanostat–muscle force coupling: weight-bearing exercise
  simultaneously drives muscle hypertrophy (mTOR) and bone formation
  (WNT/sclerostin suppression); GH/IGF-1 and sex hormones regulate both
  systems in parallel through shared anabolic receptors

- Exercise–brain endocrine axis: four parallel channels (BDNF, irisin,
  lactate, osteocalcin) converge on hippocampal neuroplasticity and
  monoamine synthesis; no single pharmacological agent currently
  activates all four simultaneously; regular exercise is among the most
  mechanistically complete antidepressant and cognitive aging
  interventions currently known

- Metabolic interface: skeletal muscle accounts for ~70% of
  insulin-stimulated glucose uptake; AMPK-driven GLUT4 translocation
  bypasses insulin signaling defects; osteocalcin enhances β-cell
  secretion; lactate–Cori cycle coupling fuels brain during
  high-intensity exercise and drives cerebral angiogenesis over time

When one node fails, the coupling architecture amplifies rather than
corrects: sarcopenia reduces bone loading → osteocalcin declines →
muscle mitochondrial function worsens → further sarcopenia. Bone loss
reduces osteocalcin–β-cell coupling → insulin secretion impaired →
metabolic syndrome worsens. Inactivity reduces BDNF, irisin, and
osteocalcin simultaneously → hippocampal neurogenesis falls → depression
risk ↑ → motivation for exercise ↓ (D2R decline) → further inactivity.
The self-amplifying nature of the sedentary aging cascade is the
mechanistic reason that exercise interventions must be initiated early:
once sarcopenia and osteocalcin decline are established, the
motivational substrate for exercise recovery is compromised.
Interventions that engage the coupling architecture simultaneously —
resistance training (muscle + bone + osteocalcin), aerobic training
(BDNF + irisin + lactate + AMPK), adequate protein and calcium (mTOR +
bone mineralization) — reverse all nodes concurrently.

**§6 — Scope, Limitations, and Companion References**

This paper proposes a coupled endocrine framework for the
musculoskeletal system organized around four shared coupling interfaces.
Three limitations apply.

First, evidence confidence varies substantially across the paper. The
AMPK–PGC-1α axis, RANKL/OPG system, mechanostat, and their clinical
consequences (sarcopenia, osteoporosis, fracture risk) are Tier A —
among the most thoroughly characterized mechanisms in physiology. The
irisin receptor mechanism (now Tier B following 2023 cryo-EM structural
confirmation), BDNF–muscle connection (Tier B with 2024 human data), and
osteocalcin–bone–brain axis (Tier B mechanistic; Tier C for human
clinical magnitude) are supported but not yet at the level of
interventional human trial confirmation. The sedentary aging–cognitive
decline cascade and GLP-1 lean mass mitigation strategies are Tier C
integrative syntheses. Readers should apply those tiers when evaluating
clinical implications.

Second, several important areas are outside the scope of this paper: the
neuromuscular junction detailed architecture (covered in PeripheralNS
v0.8 §2.5), bone marrow hematopoiesis and the immune-bone interface
(covered in OrganSystem_Immune), cardiac muscle physiology (covered in
CardiovascularRenal v1.3), and the detailed pharmacology of anabolic
agents beyond the agents mentioned as mechanism validators.

Third, the failure cascades in §4 are integrative syntheses. Each
component mechanism is supported at the tier indicated; the cascades as
unified clinical patterns are proposed rather than directly measured.
They are intended to identify the shared mechanistic architecture
underlying musculoskeletal and metabolic disease, not to substitute for
clinical guidelines on their management.
