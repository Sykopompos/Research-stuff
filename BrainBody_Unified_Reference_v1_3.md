**§1 — The Core Argument: One System, Not Two**

The brain-body system is a distributed detection and response
architecture. Six primary highways link brain and body continuously;
five are bidirectional in signaling, and the glymphatic system provides
a largely unidirectional waste-clearance route active predominantly
during sleep. The brain detects body state; the body detects brain
state. Hormones are not passengers on those highways — they are primary
operating signals that set detection thresholds, gate responses, and
determine what the brain can and cannot do. Medicine has treated the
brain as separable from the body, and hormones as separable from
neuroscience. Neither separation reflects the architecture. The
component mechanisms assembled here are not new discoveries — they are
distributed across the neuroendocrinology, neuroimmunology, and
psychoneuroimmunology literature, cited throughout. The contribution of
this paper is operational specificity: a complete, cross-referenced
wiring diagram granular enough to function as a simulation architecture
specification — not a theoretical framework but a parameter inventory.
The resolution and cross-system integration in a single reference is
what is new, not the existence of a unifying view. Four facts establish
that the brain-body boundary is a classification artifact, not a
biological reality:

- THE VAGUS NERVE IS PREDOMINANTLY AFFERENT (~80% body→brain fibers,
  ~20% efferent). The body communicates with the brain far more than the
  brain communicates with the body \[1\]. The vagus is primarily a
  body-to-brain reporting cable — carrying signals from gut
  mechanoreceptors, chemoreceptors, baroreceptors, pulmonary stretch
  receptors, and immune cytokine sensors to the brainstem \[2\].
  Everything the body senses travels up this highway before the brain
  has any chance to respond. Perception of the body is the foundation of
  every cognitive state.

- 90% OF THE BODY'S SEROTONIN IS IN THE GUT. The neurotransmitter most
  associated with mood and psychiatric treatment is produced almost
  entirely in the gut by enterochromaffin cells \[3,34\]. Gut dysbiosis
  shifts tryptophan metabolism toward the kynurenine pathway instead of
  serotonin synthesis \[31\]. The mechanism: microbiome composition
  affects plasma tryptophan availability → tryptophan crosses the BBB
  independently → dorsal raphe TPH2 uses it as substrate → brain 5-HT
  synthesis. Peripheral 5-HT from enterochromaffin cells does not cross
  the BBB; gut 5-HT acts on vagal 5-HT3 afferents for satiety and
  motility signalling, terminating at the brainstem. The gut-to-brain
  serotonin link is a tryptophan availability story, not a direct 5-HT
  supply chain. The psychiatrist prescribing an SSRI is treating a brain
  that runs on precursors whose availability the gut determines.

- SICKNESS BEHAVIOR IS A BRAIN PROGRAM, NOT A BODY SYMPTOM. When the
  immune system activates, IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α signal the brain via
  circumventricular organs and vagal afferents \[6,8\]. The brain then
  runs a specific program: fatigue, anhedonia, social withdrawal,
  cognitive slowing, increased pain sensitivity, reduced appetite. This
  sickness behavior is not a side effect of infection — it is an
  adaptive program that redirects metabolic resources from energetically
  expensive cognition toward immune activity \[6\]. Medicine suppresses
  it as if it were a malfunction. It is a feature.

- HORMONES ARE A BRAIN OPERATING SYSTEM, NOT A REPRODUCTIVE SIGNAL.
  Estradiol amplifies hippocampal LTP 1.5-3× via two distinct timescales
  of action \[11,12\]. Allopregnanolone is the brain's endogenous GABA-A
  positive allosteric modulator \[13,14\]. DHEA is the brain's
  anti-glucocorticoid buffer \[17\] — experimental data show DHEA/DHEAS
  can counter some glucocorticoid effects on hippocampal LTP and
  neurotoxicity; the DHEA/cortisol ratio is an emerging
  stress-resilience indicator, though causal strength in humans remains
  under active study. The brain synthesizes its own versions of all of
  these from local cholesterol, independently of the gonads \[13,14\].
  When ovarian production stops at menopause, or testicular production
  declines in andropause, the brain loses critical operating signals.
  The cognitive, mood, sleep, and pain consequences are mechanistically
  direct.

**§2 — The Six Primary Brain-Body Highways**

Five primary highways carry information bidirectionally between brain
and body — the HPG axis (sex hormones) adds a sixth that medicine has
categorized as reproductive rather than neural, obscuring its profound
brain-modulating function \[11,12\]. The glymphatic system provides a
seventh pathway, largely unidirectional: waste clearance from brain
interstitium during sleep. The five signaling highways are
bidirectional; all operate continuously. Disruption of any one produces
consequences in both directions simultaneously.

*All six systems interact continuously. Chronic stress (HPA) reduces
gonadal hormone production (stress-induced amenorrhea in women,
testosterone reduction in men). Gonadal hormone loss reduces
neuroinflammatory buffering (E2, T anti-inflammatory effects) →
immune-brain axis dysregulation. Sleep disruption reduces glymphatic
clearance, worsens neuroinflammation, and further impairs gonadal
hormone production. One dysregulated highway cascades through all
others.*

**§4 — The Hormonal Brain: Two Timescales of Action**

Sex hormones and neurosteroids act on the brain through two
fundamentally different timescales that produce different types of
effects. The fast membrane effects \[11\] operate on the timescale of
synaptic transmission — changing cognitive state within minutes, without
gene transcription. The slow genomic effects \[12\] reshape synaptic
topology over hours to days — explaining why months of hormone therapy
produce MRI-visible structural changes, and why long-term hormone
deprivation produces structural cognitive consequences.

The postpartum day-1 mood crash, the pre-menstrual hour-scale emotional
shift, and the week-scale cognitive changes following HRT initiation or
cessation — all are fast membrane effects of hormone withdrawal
operating below the threshold of gene transcription. Medicine calibrated
to the slow genomic timescale misses half of the hormonal brain's
operating dynamics.

**§5 — Neurosteroids: The Brain's Internal Hormone Factory**

The brain is not merely a target for peripheral hormones — it
synthesizes its own steroid hormones from cholesterol, independently of
the gonads and adrenal glands \[13,14\]. These neurosteroids modulate
synaptic transmission at the local synapse level, operate independently
of circulating hormone levels, and represent a pharmacological system
that medicine has largely overlooked because it is invisible to standard
blood hormone panels.

The neurosteroid synthesis chain: Cholesterol → Pregnenolone
(rate-limiting step, in brain mitochondria via StAR and CYP11A1) →
branches to DHEA, Progesterone → Allopregnanolone, and via aromatase
(CYP19A1) to local Estradiol \[13,19\]. Every step of this chain occurs
within neurons and astrocytes. The same steroid hormones the gonads
produce are being synthesized at the synapse level in response to local
neural activity. This is a primary synaptic modulation system, not a
backup.

**5.1 — The Measurement Problem: Why Standard Panels Cannot See the
Neurosteroid System**

Standard blood sex hormone panels (E2, T, P4) measure peripheral
circulating levels. Neurosteroid levels in brain tissue and CSF are not
reliably reflected in blood \[13,14\]. A patient with 'normal' blood E2
can have disrupted local hippocampal neurosteroid synthesis — the
aromatase activity that produces local brain E2 from T is regulated
independently of ovarian E2 production \[12\]. A patient with clinically
normal blood progesterone can have PMDD driven by GABA-A subunit
sensitivity to ALLO fluctuation — the pathology is in the receptor, not
in the hormone level \[15\]. The DHEA/cortisol ratio that determines
hippocampal stress vulnerability cannot be assessed from either value
alone — both must be measured and their ratio calculated \[17\].
Clinical practice is managing a pharmacological system it largely cannot
measure at the relevant resolution.

**§6 — Hormones Across the Lifespan: Brain Consequences at Every Stage**

The hormonal environment of the brain changes more dramatically across
the human lifespan than any other physiological system. Fetal
organizational effects permanently establish brain architecture \[36\].
Puberty transforms the operating system. Peak reproductive years provide
the maximum neuroprotective buffer. Perimenopause and andropause
represent the removal of that buffer — a whole-brain state change, not a
reproductive transition. Elderhood operates in a near-depletion
environment in which neurosteroid production may be 10-20% of peak
\[17,18\].

Every major increase in psychiatric vulnerability — first depression at
puberty, postpartum crisis, perimenopausal depression peak, male
mid-life anhedonia — corresponds to a hormonal transition point
\[15,28\]. These are not coincidences. They are the signature of a brain
operating on hormonal signals encountering transitions in those signals.

**§7 — Sex Differences in Brain Function: The Hormonal Mechanisms**

Documented sex differences in cognition, pain, mood, stress response,
and disease vulnerability are real on a population level \[27,28,29\].
The majority of the mechanistic explanation lies in the hormonal
differences between male and female brains — not in irreducible
biological 'hardwiring.' The same differences can be partially
reproduced pharmacologically by hormone administration and partially
reversed by hormone withdrawal. They are operating parameters, not fixed
categories. These are population-level distributions with very large
individual overlap — they are not predictive for individuals, and
social, cultural, and environmental factors interact substantially with
the hormonal substrate throughout life.

the correct response to this evidence base, not the assumption that
average effects apply uniformly across hormonal states.*

**§11 — Clinical Implications: What Changes When You See the System**

**11.1 — Neurosteroid Withdrawal Is a Primary Mechanism in a Substantial
Subset of PPD (~15% Fully ALLO-Responsive)**

Brexanolone (synthetic allopregnanolone, IV infusion) achieves 70%+
remission in postpartum depression with effects beginning within hours —
faster than any antidepressant class \[15\]. Zuranolone (oral ALLO
analog) received FDA approval in 2023 \[16\]. SSRIs take 4-6 weeks for a
condition that has a mechanism-matched, rapid-acting treatment. The
mechanism: progesterone collapses at delivery → allopregnanolone
collapses → one of the most rapid neurosteroid drops in adult life
occurs within 24-72 hours of birth \[15\]. PPD is heterogeneous and not
all cases are neurosteroid-driven, but brexanolone and zuranolone trial
results establish that the neurosteroid-responsive subset is
substantial. Third-trimester screening to identify ALLO-sensitive
individuals is a research-agenda proposal consistent with the mechanism
— not yet an established clinical protocol. The treatment exists. The
clinical pathway has not yet caught up.

**11.2 — Perimenopause Is a Neurological Transition, Not a Reproductive
One**

The HRT decision at menopause is currently framed primarily around
breast cancer risk and cardiovascular safety. The neurological dimension
— hippocampal plasticity loss, sleep disruption, pain threshold
reduction, 5-HT system destabilization, amyloid protection removal — is
rarely the center of the conversation \[25,26\]. The 10-year critical
window (HRT initiated within 10 years of menopause onset shows cognitive
protection; initiated after does not \[25,26\]) suggests the
neurological dimension should be considered early — before symptoms
severe enough to prompt specialist referral appear, and while the
hormonal transition is still reversible. Formulation is not neutral:
transdermal estradiol with micronized progesterone carries a more
favorable cardiovascular and breast cancer risk profile than oral
conjugated estrogen with synthetic progestins in most comparative
analyses — the neurological case for HRT should be made in the context
of formulation-specific risk. Evidence on this timing hypothesis
continues to evolve, and individual risk-benefit assessment
incorporating APOE4 status, cardiovascular baseline, formulation choice,
and personal history is required \[25,26,29\]. Importantly, trials and
analyses with null or adverse cognitive outcomes exist — particularly
with late-life initiation and oral conjugated estrogen formulations —
meaning any simulation model of HRT effects should allow for protective,
neutral, and adverse outcomes depending on timing and formulation
parameters, not assume a uniformly beneficial effect. But the
neurological case must be part of that conversation.

**11.3 — Free Testosterone Is a Missing Variable in Male Psychiatric
Assessment**

The mechanistic rationale for free testosterone assessment in male
depression is strong; the comparative-effectiveness evidence — large
RCTs powered for depression endpoints comparing T-guided pathways
against usual care — is limited. This section is a proposal for a
clinical research agenda, not a current guideline. For men over 40
presenting with depression, anhedonia, reduced motivation, irritability,
or cognitive slowing: free testosterone (not total T — SHBG-bound T is
inactive) measurement is a mechanistically grounded addition to the
initial workup \[28,36\]. Low free T → 5-HT reduction (AR on raphe) + DA
tone reduction (striatal) + DHEA/cortisol ratio worsening + spatial
memory decline \[17,36\]. Testosterone replacement in truly hypogonadal
depressed men produces antidepressant effects independently of libido
effects — this is a 5-HT + DA mechanism, not a psychological one \[28\].
The current standard of care measures TSH and checks B12 but does not
routinely measure free T; whether correcting this produces superior
depression outcomes requires prospective trial evidence.

**11.4 — The Neurosteroid System Is a Pharmacological Target Medicine
Has Barely Touched**

Brexanolone \[15\] and zuranolone \[16\] are the first medications that
directly target the neurosteroid system rather than the monoamine
system. DHEA shows antidepressant effects in randomized trials, with
variable effect sizes across populations and dose protocols — evidence
is promising but not yet definitive for routine prescription \[17\].
Pregnenolone shows cognitive improvement for schizophrenia negative
symptoms in controlled trials \[19\]. The sigma-1 receptor (pregnenolone
target) is being actively investigated for pain, depression, and
neurodegenerative disease. The neurosteroid system represents arguably
the most underdeveloped pharmacological target in psychiatry — now with
two FDA-approved medications establishing proof of concept.

**11.5 — Glymphatic Health Is a Hormonal Health Problem**

Deep NREM sleep — the primary driver of glymphatic function — is
dependent on ALLO (GABA-A-mediated sleep pressure \[13\]) and body
temperature regulation (E2-dependent in women). E2 and P4 loss at
menopause disrupts both: ALLO falls, removing the GABAergic
sleep-pressure mechanism; E2 loss destabilizes hypothalamic
thermoregulation, producing hot flashes that fragment sleep \[5,35\].
The result: glymphatic failure → adenosine accumulation → daytime
cognitive impairment → accelerated amyloid deposition \[5,35\]. HRT that
restores ALLO tone (including natural progesterone, which converts to
ALLO \[15\]) and reduces hot flashes directly improves sleep
architecture and, by extension, glymphatic function — a mechanistically
coherent reason to consider hormonal management as part of dementia
prevention.

**11.6 — Exercise: The Most Broadly Supported Neuroprotective
Intervention**

The mechanism is specific: aerobic exercise → muscle releases IGF-1
\[21\] → crosses BBB → drives hippocampal BDNF expression \[20\] → DG
neurogenesis + synaptic density increase. Concurrent exercise-induced
IL-6 (from muscle, anti-inflammatory at this context) drives
anti-inflammatory cascades \[8\]. For mild-to-moderate depression,
several large meta-analyses find effect sizes for aerobic exercise in
the same range as antidepressant pharmacotherapy, though estimates vary
by severity, population, and adherence \[20\]. Observational evidence
for dementia risk reduction is more consistent than for any
pharmacological intervention studied to date — though this evidence base
is predominantly epidemiological rather than RCT-derived, and
confounding cannot be fully excluded \[20,21\]. Exercise is arguably the
most broadly supported non-pharmacological neuroprotective intervention
we have, with converging mechanistic, clinical, and epidemiological
evidence; head-to-head randomized comparisons with other candidate
interventions remain limited. It is the most underprescribed
intervention in medicine. The evidence base supports elevating it to
first-line status; that case is not yet fully reflected in most current
guidelines.

**§12 — Conclusion: The Hormonal Brain Was Always There**

The brain is a steroid-sensitive, neurosteroid-synthesizing,
hormone-modulated organ that changes its operating parameters at every
major hormonal transition across the lifespan. The individual mechanisms
assembled in this paper have existed in the literature for decades —
distributed across reproductive endocrinology, neuroscience, psychiatry,
pain medicine, and immunology journals that rarely read each other. The
synthesis has been missing.

What changes when you add the hormonal layer to the brain-body picture:

- Postpartum depression gains a neurosteroid withdrawal model — a
  mechanism-matched treatment (brexanolone/zuranolone) achieves
  remission in hours for a substantial responsive subset, establishing
  proof of concept that ALLO withdrawal is a primary driver in at least
  a large fraction of cases, not a universal serotonin deficiency.

- Perimenopausal depression, insomnia, pain, and cognitive change become
  a whole-brain hormonal transition requiring early, timed intervention
  — not a collection of symptoms managed by separate specialists.

- Male depression becomes partially a testosterone and DHEA deficiency
  disorder with measurable, treatable substrate — not uniformly a
  serotonin problem.

- Sex differences in pain, Alzheimer's risk, autoimmune vulnerability,
  and stress reactivity become mechanistically explained by hormonal
  operating parameter differences — not fixed biological categories.

- Neurosteroids (ALLO, DHEA, pregnenolone) become a pharmacological
  system as important as the monoamine system — with two FDA-approved
  medications establishing proof of concept and the field beginning to
  develop.

- Glymphatic health becomes a hormonal health problem — sleep disruption
  at menopause is mechanistically hormonal and requires hormonal as well
  as sleep-hygiene solutions.

The brain and body are one system. Hormones are one of its primary
operating signals. The conversation between brain and body runs through
gonadal and adrenal chemistry as much as it runs through
neurotransmitters. Medicine that treats the brain as separable from the
body, and hormones as separable from neuroscience, is working with a
model that doesn't match the system it is treating.

The model that does match the system has been assembled here from
existing literature. It was always there. The specialty borders just
made it hard to see all at once.

*The brain makes its own hormones. The body makes its own
neurotransmitters. There is no border. There never was.*

*Note on simulation use: A.1 variables with 'years' timescales are
slowly-varying baselines that set the operating range of faster
variables — menopause_stage drives E2_level and ALLO_level, which then
modulate LTP_gain and GABA_tonic_inhibition across all faster
timescales. Variables with 'minutes-hours' timescales fluctuate
continuously within any observation window. Derived variables
(DHEA_cortisol_ratio, free_T_level, brain_insulin_resistance_index,
microglial_M1_M2_ratio) are computed from their inputs rather than
directly set. Integral expressions (amyloid_load, tau_load) represent
cumulative trajectories, not instantaneous states. Numeric ranges are
order-of-magnitude anchors; primary sources should be consulted for
precise parameterization.*
