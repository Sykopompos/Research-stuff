# Brain-Body Simulation Reference Library



A personal project. I'm designing a brain/body simulation and needed accurate, cross-system biological parameters to work from. The problem is that most biological research is siloed — neuroscience, immunology, endocrinology, and metabolic biology all study the same body from separate windows and rarely synthesize across them. For a simulation that models the whole system, that siloing breaks things. These documents are my attempt to detangle it.



The BrainBody Unified Reference is the core document — a cross-system wiring diagram treating the brain and body as one integrated architecture. The NeurochemFormula Correspondence maps every canonical neurotransmitter and hormone formula to its operating parameters. The disease papers apply the same framework to specific conditions.

Everything here is in active development. Papers get updated as new systems get mapped — cross-system connections get added back into existing documents as the full picture fills in. The more of the body that gets covered, the more accurate each individual paper becomes. Check back.



A dozen additional papers covering body systems and disease mechanisms are in progress — being updated against the current reference documents, cross-checked for consistency, and verified for accuracy and citation quality before they go up. They are uploaded because the cross-system integrated view doesn't exist anywhere else in one place, and that gap leaves researchers, clinicians, and patients working from incomplete pictures. If any of it helps someone, that's reason enough to share.



The goal is to map the whole body as one cross-system reference — every system accounted for, every connection documented, nothing siloed. Papers in progress cover cognitive profiles (ADHD, autism spectrum), endocrine system, limbic system, autonomic and enteric nervous systems, and additional disease and condition papers. All are held until they meet the same standard as what is already here: accurate, consistently cross-referenced, and properly cited.





**What this is building toward**

The reference library is ground truth for two projects that share the same biological foundation.

**Interactive brain and body simulation with disease modeling**

An interactive cross-system simulation of the full brain and body — accurate enough to model disease states, treatment responses, and system interactions across all relevant subsystems simultaneously rather than in isolation. The reference library here is the biological foundation that makes that possible.

This kind of cross-system simulation has direct applications in drug development, disease modeling, surgical planning, medical education, and neuroscience research. This is actively being worked toward.



**Novel cognitive architecture**

A biologically grounded cognitive architecture — not a neural network, not a language model, not any variant of existing machine learning approaches — one that learns continuously from structured curriculum rather than from expensive training runs on frozen weights, and adapts through edge-level mechanisms rather than retraining. The design goal is not an oracle you query for answers. It is an on-device symbiotic partner — a system that works alongside a person, adapts to how they think, fills in where they have gaps, and teaches rather than just answers. Both the person and the system grow through the relationship. The biology has to be right because the architecture is built directly from it.

The disease papers in the reference library also serve as biological ground truth for the architecture's threat detection and response system — the digital equivalent of an immune system, built from the same biology as the conditions it needs to recognize.

Both are personal projects at this stage — no company, no funding. If either of these is interesting to you as a collaborator, a researcher, or someone who knows where serious independent work like this can find support — feel free to reach out.

**Tools used:** Primary writing and research synthesis via Claude.ai; citation verification and deep research cross-checked through Perplexity AI across multiple underlying models. All AI-generated content is verified independently — claims are checked against primary literature, outputs are verified for internal consistency and accuracy, and anything that doesn't hold up gets corrected or cut. AI tools are prone to hallucination; the verification process is what separates this from generated slop. The work is mine — the tools make it possible to do it without an institution behind me.





**Why it looks like academic papers**

Two reasons. First, that format is the most useful for organizing mechanistic claims with citations — every assertion is attached to its evidence, which makes verification straightforward. It's harder to introduce vague overclaims when every sentence has to be backed by something specific.

Second, Claude.ai — the primary writing tool used here — naturally produces well-structured academic-style output. That turns out to be an advantage: citation checking and accuracy verification are easier when the format makes every claim explicit and traceable. The format is a feature, not an affectation.
